Biography

Jane Inskip was born in 1766 in Bolton by Bowland, a small village in the Ribble Valley of the Yorkshire Dales in northern England. She married Benjamin Harrison on 27 July 1784 in that same village, beginning a partnership that would eventually carry their family across an ocean to build new lives in the American wilderness.

The name she carried into marriage — Inskip — was not forgotten. It would be given to her son, who became Inskip Harrison, one of the foundational figures of the Rome Township community. From him it passed to Edward Inskip Harrison, and eventually crossed into the Morris family through marriage, appearing as late as Harold Inskip Morris in the twentieth century. Jane Inskip Harrison may never have known how far her name would travel through time.

At some point after their marriage Jane and Benjamin Harrison left England for America, joining the wave of English settlers who made their way to northwestern Pennsylvania in the early nineteenth century. The precise year of their immigration is not yet confirmed, but by the time their children were establishing families of their own, the Harrisons were deeply rooted in what would become Rome Township — a community that would eventually bear their name.

Jane Inskip Harrison died on 13 October 1845. She was among the earliest burials in what would become English Settlement Cemetery, and her stone marks the beginning of the Harrison family's long presence there. She was born before the American Revolution. She died the year James K. Polk was President. In between she crossed an ocean, raised six children, and helped found a community in Crawford County, Pennsylvania.

Jane Inskip Harrison is the earliest documented birth in English Settlement Cemetery (1766) and is almost certainly the origin of the Inskip name that appears across four generations of the Harrison and Morris families. Her marriage record in Bolton by Bowland, Yorkshire provides the earliest confirmed geographic origin for the Harrison family's English roots. Bolton by Bowland remains a small village in the Ribble Valley today.
